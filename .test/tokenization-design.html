Act 01 · The Design

The shape of the design.

Context · The Problem

We need to protect sensitive data in motion and at rest, without breaking the applications that use it.

The platform tokenizes regulated fields (SSN, account numbers, and adjacent data classes) so that downstream systems can operate on reversible, format-preserving tokens instead of plaintext. The architecture has to perform at the rate of a function call. It has to survive the compromise of any single component. And it has to satisfy FHFA examiners on key custody and audit.

Those three requirements pull in different directions. The codebook model is how we honor all three in the same design.

Primitive · The Shape Of The Problem

Two in-process tokenization primitives, each reversible to whoever holds the key.

Pre-computed codebook · in-process lookup
  • PCI-SSC Type 1. Per-n-gram substitution tables (2–3 chars), encrypted under a master key and shipped to every consumer. Tables stay small (100 entries for pairs, 1,000 for triples), but substitution is positional: plaintext frequency survives into the token, and repeated n-grams produce repeated outputs.
Cryptographic tokenization · reversible by key
  • FF1 format-preserving encryption over AES-256. A bijection over the full value, keyed by 32 bytes, with a domain-specific tweak. No plaintext vault, no lookup on the hot path, O(1) rotation.

FF1 wins because positional n-gram substitution leaks plaintext frequency into the token. A full-value bijection does not.

Primitive · The FF1 Patent Posture

FF1's patent history is a licensing question, not an architectural risk.

What the patents claim
  • Voltage Security (now OpenText, via HPE and Micro Focus) holds patents on format-preserving encryption techniques underlying FF1 and FFX, notably US 8,948,376 and adjacent filings. NIST SP 800-38G standardizes FF1 with a RAND licensing commitment from the patent holder. The core claims cover the FFX construction, tweak derivation, and the Feistel round structure that FF1 inherits.
❯
Where we stand
  • Our implementation is a NIST SP 800-38G-conformant FF1 library over AES-256, drawn from the open-source ecosystem with broad financial-services adoption. The earliest primary patents in the family begin expiring in the 2024-2027 window; by Phase 2 of this platform, the most load-bearing claims are public domain. We treat the current window as a licensing question to settle with procurement, not an architectural risk. The primitive choice does not change if a license is required, and the library choice is swappable across any conformant implementation.

FF1 remains the right primitive because the alternative is not a different cipher, it is a different architecture. FPE is what allows tokens to fit the schema of the systems they replace. Non-format-preserving encryption would force downstream system changes across every consumer. That cost is strictly larger than a license fee, and it is the cost the patent posture would impose on a platform that tried to avoid the question.

The codebook is three things at once: time-bound, signed, and identity-bound.

Time-bound and revocable
  • Every codebook carries a TTL and a version. The control plane can force a refresh, raise a version floor, or revoke a single client's codebook without redeploying any consuming application. A compromised codebook expires on its own, and can be killed faster than it expires.
Signed and tamper-evident
  • The codebook is Ed25519-signed by the control plane. The SDK verifies the signature on receipt and rejects anything that does not match the pinned public key. Policy, schema, and wrapped DEK all live inside the signed envelope. Permissions, keys, and schemas cannot be altered in transit without invalidating the signature.
Identity-bound and non-transferable
  • Tenant ID and client ID sit inside the signed envelope and are verified by the SDK against the calling application's identity. The codebook ID itself is additional authenticated data on the wrapped DEK. A codebook transplanted to the wrong application fails cryptographically, not by policy rejection.

The KEK lives in hardware. The DEK lives in native memory for one operation.

The KEK never leaves CloudHSM. It is a non-exportable hardware key. Every unwrap operation is logged by the HSM itself, outside the application's control and outside the SDK's audit boundary. That independence is what makes the log tamper-evident: it exists whether or not the audited component is honest.

The DEK lives in the codebook as ciphertext and in native memory as plaintext for the duration of one operation. The SDK unwraps the DEK into a direct ByteBuffer outside the JVM heap, mlocks it against swap, performs FF1, and zeros it on AutoCloseable close. The DEK never touches disk. Garbage collection never sees it.

The SDK is five components, each with one job.

  1. Codebook Loader
    • Parses the signed payload, verifies the Ed25519 signature against the pinned public key, caches the result. Hard-rejects anything that does not verify.
  2. DEK Manager
    • Unwraps the DEK into native memory per operation, enforces the AutoCloseable lifecycle, zeroes the buffer on close. Never hands a plaintext DEK to application code.
  3. Character Mapper and Tweak Deriver
    • Encodes plaintext to FF1's integer sequence and derives the domain-specific tweak via HMAC. Handles passthrough characters positionally. It is the adapter between real-world data and the cipher.
  4. FF1 Engine
    • NIST SP 800-38G FF1 over AES-256, drawn from a conformant open-source library. Consumes the DEK and the tweak. This is the cipher; everything else in the list is plumbing around it.
  5. Policy Enforcer and Audit Emitter
    • Enforces the codebook's embedded policy before FF1 runs. Tokenize-only clients cannot detokenize. Wrong tenant ID hard-fails. Emits structured audit events for every operation.

The control plane is the platform's operating surface.

What it owns
  • User and role management. Client certificate provisioning. Per-domain policy definition. Codebook generation and signing. Version floor and revocation management. Audit of every issuance and policy change. Everything the consuming applications see originates here.
What it does not own
  • It never holds plaintext DEKs. KEKs live in CloudHSM. The control plane requests unwrap operations from the HSM to assemble codebooks; it does not retain the result. The separation is deliberate. A compromise of the control plane does not, by itself, expose any DEK.
Design Summary · The One-Finding View

The codebook model gets the performance of in-process tokenization with the key custody of a vault-integrated system.

What the architecture gets right

In-process tokenize and detokenize at function-call latency. Key material is never at rest unwrapped. The HSM-anchored audit trail survives compromise of any application component. Revocation is measurable in minutes, not days. Per-tenant isolation is enforced by cryptography, not by policy.

What the architecture asks of us

CloudHSM must be available at codebook issuance and refresh time. That is the refresh cadence, not the hot path, but it is still a dependency. The SDK must be implemented correctly across every language target we support. The control plane is a system we operate; it is not a vendor-managed service.

●The design honors speed, resilience, and examination readiness in the same architecture, without trading any one against the others.

Act 02 · The Runway

How this ships.

Principle · What We Defer, We Do Not Rebuild

The architecture ships in three phases, and no phase invalidates the one before it.

The instinct to ship fast and fix later is usually how platforms accumulate the debt that kills them at eighteen months. This deck argues for the opposite discipline: ship narrow, but lock in the primitives every later phase needs. Each phase extends the one before it instead of replacing it. Every choice in Phase 1 is evaluated not just on Phase 1 fit, but on whether Phase 2 and Phase 3 can build on it without rework.

The runway is three phases, each with a clear promise.

  1. Ship the architecture, prove the pattern
    • One field class, one tenant, new writes only. The codebook model works end to end. The SDK integrates with one consuming application. Manual rotation. Version-floor revocation. Production-grade, but narrow.
  2. Scale the scope, harden the operations
    • Multi-tenant and full field-class coverage. Historical data migration. Automated rotation. The transformation pipeline becomes a first-class capability. Crypto-shred on tenant offboarding.
  3. Open the boundaries
    • Non-prod egress, external vendor scoping, analytics-plane detokenize gates. Per-purpose codebooks. The platform becomes the boundary between regulated data and every system that is not the source of truth.

Phase 01 · What Ships

Phase 1 is deliberately narrow. One field, one tenant, new writes.

One field class tokenized end to end. One tenant configured in the control plane. One consuming application integrated with the SDK. The codebook model is fully implemented: Ed25519 signing, mTLS distribution, TTL refresh, version-floor revocation. CloudHSM is integrated and the KEK is non-exportable. The audit trail is live on day one.

What is not in Phase 1: historical migration, automated rotation, multi-tenant operations, non-prod flows, vendor codebooks. Those arrive in Phase 2 and Phase 3.

Phase 1 is narrow in scope, but not narrow in primitives.

DEK versioning is in the design from the first codebook. Every codebook carries a version. Every token produced is traceable to the codebook version that produced it. The engineering cost in Phase 1 is small. The payoff is that Phase 2 can add rotation and historical migration without re-keying anything already in production.

The codebook schema is multi-DEK-ready. Phase 1 uses one DEK per codebook. The schema supports multiple DEKs per codebook, one per domain or one per purpose, and the SDK resolves the correct DEK at operation time. Phase 2 activates that path without changing the schema or invalidating Phase 1 tokens.

Phase 02 · What Scales

Phase 2 extends scope and introduces the transformation pipeline.

Multi-tenant operation. Full field-class coverage: SSN, account numbers, DOB, email, and the other regulated classes the platform holds. Per-tenant-per-field-class DEKs activated, using the schema already in place. Automated rotation on a scheduled cadence, driven by the transformation pipeline. Historical data migration completes in this phase, also through the pipeline.

Phase 2 is the transition from a working integration to an operational platform. Nothing built in Phase 1 is thrown away. The SDK, the codebooks, the tokens already in production all continue to work without re-keying.

Phase 03 · What Opens

Phase 3 turns the platform into a boundary.

Non-prod environments receive tokenize-only or read-only codebooks. Plaintext never flows to dev, test, or staging. External vendors that need to process regulated data receive scoped codebooks with narrow field coverage, short TTLs, and aggressive audit. Analytics and reporting systems see tokenized columns by default, with per-user per-field detokenize gates for the narrow cases where plaintext is legitimately required.

In Phase 3, the codebook model becomes the boundary between the systems of record and everything else. The differentiation between a prod consumer, a non-prod consumer, and a vendor is enforced by cryptography. Each consumer holds the codebook that authorizes exactly what it needs, and cannot reach anything else.

The phasing is what makes this defensible to both leadership and examiners.

Phase 1 ships the architecture, not the full platform

That is what leadership asked for, delivered with the primitives that make everything else possible. The executive sees working tokenization integrated with a real application. The examiner sees HSM-anchored key custody and a tamper-evident audit trail. Both answers are real at first delivery.

Phase 2 closes the operational story

Historical migration, automated rotation, crypto-shred. The transformation pipeline is built once and serves every use case that involves re-keying data in bulk. By the end of Phase 2, the platform is something examiners can certify against.

Phase 3 makes data boundaries cryptographic

Prod, non-prod, and external systems each hold different codebooks with different capabilities. The boundary is a property of the key, enforced at issuance.

What Phase 1 could teach us that would change the design.

  1. TTL refresh cadence may not fit every application pattern
    • The current design assumes all clients tolerate a background refresh at 80% of TTL. Batch workloads, short-lived jobs, and cold-start-heavy services may need a different refresh model. If Phase 1 surfaces this, the refresh policy becomes per-client rather than global. That is an additive change, but one we would rather know about early.
  2. CloudHSM unwrap latency under load may force pre-fetching
    • Codebook issuance is off the hot path, but it is on the refresh path. If steady-state unwrap latency is higher than modeled under concurrent load, we may need to pre-warm DEKs in the control plane or introduce a wrapped-DEK cache. The architecture supports both. The question is whether we actually need them.
  3. Per-field-class DEK operational cost may exceed the isolation benefit
    • We have modeled per-tenant-per-field-class DEKs as a reasonable tradeoff. If Phase 1 shows the key lifecycle work (rotations, revocations, policy changes) multiplies linearly with domains in a way our operations team cannot absorb, we may need to consolidate domains within a tenant. The transformation pipeline makes that possible. It is a real engineering cost we would rather avoid.

Act 03 · The Choices

Every architecture is a set of bets. These are ours.

The four bets ahead

This act walks through the choices that most shape the platform. Each one has a name, a cost, and a payoff. None is reversed cheaply once tokens exist in production.

  1. Key hierarchy
    • Per-tenant-per-field-class DEKs under a KEK partitioned inside a shared CloudHSM cluster.
  2. Rotation posture
    • Version-floor over hard cutover; rotation is a floor increment, not a coordinated redeploy.
  3. Offline tolerance
    • The SDK works on a cached codebook until the TTL runs out, and no further.
  4. Audit posture
    • Three independent layers; the HSM layer is the one load-bearing against examiner scrutiny.

Key hierarchy: we chose per-tenant-per-field-class DEKs.

The choice
  • Each tenant has a KEK in CloudHSM. Under that KEK, each data domain (SSN, account number, DOB, email) gets its own DEK. A codebook carries one or more wrapped DEKs depending on the client's authorized domains. Phase 1 ships with one DEK per codebook. Phase 2 activates the multi-DEK path using the same schema.
❯
The cost and what it buys
  • More keys means more lifecycle management: more rotations, more revocations, more wrap and unwrap operations. In exchange, a compromise of the "email tokenizer" path does not expose SSNs. Separation between domains is enforced by cryptography, and crypto-shred on tenant offboarding can be scoped per-domain rather than all-or-nothing.

Rotation posture: version-floor rather than hard cutover.

The bet we are making
  • Every codebook carries a version. The control plane broadcasts a version floor, and any codebook below the floor is treated as revoked. Rotation is a floor increment. Clients refresh on TTL and pick up the new version. No coordinated cutover, no simultaneous redeploy of every consuming application.
❯
The alternative we rejected
  • Hard-cutover rotation where every client switches at the same moment. Simpler to reason about, catastrophic when any one client fails to refresh. At our fleet size and heterogeneity, hard cutover is an availability risk that soft-rotation does not have.

The cost of our choice is that old and new codebooks coexist for the length of the TTL window. The SDK tolerates this by resolving DEK version at detokenize time. The payoff is that rotation becomes a routine operation instead of a scheduled outage.

Offline tolerance: the SDK keeps working until the TTL runs out, and no further.

The choice
  • The SDK caches its signed codebook in native memory. If the control plane is unreachable at TTL refresh time, the SDK continues to operate on the cached codebook until the TTL actually expires, and no longer. Tokenize works. Detokenize works. What does not work while offline: refreshing into a new codebook version, checking the revocation list, or picking up policy changes.
❯
The cost and what it buys
  • The cost is a bounded staleness window equal to the TTL. The payoff: the control plane stays off the hot path, brief control plane outages are invisible to consuming applications, and consuming applications are not coupled to control plane availability for their own SLOs. When the TTL expires without a refresh, the SDK hard-stops. A cached codebook is never honored past its expiry.

Audit posture: three independent layers, one load-bearing.

Layer 01 · HSM Audit
Tamper-evidentOut-of-bandExaminer-grade
Every DEK unwrap logged by CloudHSM itself, outside platform control. This is the log an examiner asks for. It survives compromise of the component being audited.
Layer 02 · Control Plane Audit
CentralizedCross-clientIn trust boundary
Codebook issuance, policy changes, revocations, cert provisioning. Rich and queryable. Lives inside the platform's trust boundary, which limits its forensic weight.
Layer 03 · SDK Local Audit

Every tokenize and detokenize emitted as a structured event in the consuming application. Useful for application-level investigation. This log lives inside the component being audited, so it is not trusted for tamper-evidence.
Layer 01 is load-bearing
Layers 2 and 3 add value. Layer 1 answers the examiner question: how do you know your audit logs have not been tampered with by a compromised application.

KEK isolation: one KEK per tenant, inside a shared HSM cluster.

The choice
  • Each tenant gets its own KEK inside CloudHSM. The cluster itself is shared across tenants; we do not operate a separate HSM per tenant. Isolation is enforced by the key, not by the hardware. CloudHSM's partition model enforces this. A tenant's KEK cannot be addressed by another tenant's credentials.
❯
What we rejected and why
  • A separate HSM cluster per tenant was considered and rejected. It does not meaningfully improve the threat model, because CloudHSM already enforces partition isolation by cryptography. And it roughly multiplies operational cost per tenant. The examiner question is the same in both cases: can tenant A's credentials reach tenant B's key material. The answer is the same: no, by hardware-enforced partition.
Choice · HSM vs KMS

CloudHSM is the default. KMS is a deployment option, not an architectural one.

CloudHSM · dedicated hardware
  • Single-tenant at the hardware boundary. FIPS 140-2 Level 3, which requires physical tamper-resistance that destroys key material on extraction attempts. We own the crypto boundary; the cluster is ours, the partitions are ours, the audit log lives inside hardware we control. The examiner question "who else's key material lives on this device" has a one-word answer: nobody.
❯
AWS KMS · managed service
  • Multi-tenant at the hardware level. FIPS 140-2 Level 2 at the API surface (Level 3 modules underneath, but the boundary the customer sees is Level 2). Simpler operations, lower cost, broader regional coverage. The examiner question is the same, but the answer requires explaining tenancy isolation as a property of AWS's HSM partitioning, not of dedicated hardware.

Design to HSM. Deploy to whichever service the risk posture permits. Phase 1 prod uses CloudHSM. Phase 3 non-prod and vendor-scoped codebooks may use KMS where the data class allows; the architecture does not change, only the issuance backend does. FIPS Level 3 requires physical tamper-resistance (key material destroyed on extraction attempt); Level 2 requires tamper-evidence (intrusion is detectable) but not destruction.

Five bets, one architecture.

We are optimizing for long-lived correctness, not short-term simplicity

Every choice in this section trades a small amount of Phase 1 engineering cost for a Phase 2 or Phase 3 capability that is expensive to retrofit. Per-field-class DEKs are more keys to manage. Version-floor rotation is more state to reason about. Multi-layer audit is more logs to produce. Each is chosen because the alternative (catching up in Phase 2) costs more than paying now.

Some choices are cheap to reverse, others are not

Version-floor rotation can be tightened to hard cutover without touching existing tokens. Offline tolerance caching can be shortened or disabled per-client. Audit layer emphasis can be retuned. The expensive choices are the cryptographic ones: alphabet ordering, tweak strategy, and domain naming are effectively one-way once tokens exist. Per-field-class DEK consolidation is possible but requires the full transformation pipeline running across the affected data. It is reversible in the same sense that rotation is reversible: through deliberate engineering, not by configuration change.

None of these choices require new capability we do not have

CloudHSM is in production. The AWS KMS patterns are familiar. Ed25519 and FF1 are well-understood primitives with reference implementations. Everything here is assembled from parts we already know how to operate.

The SDK lifecycle, part one: startup and steady state.

Startup and provisioning. The SDK loads its pinned Ed25519 public key and its mTLS client certificate from local secure storage. It makes its first codebook request to the control plane. No operation is possible until a valid, signed, unexpired codebook is in native memory. The startup is fail-closed by design. The application does not run with stale or absent key material.

Steady-state operation and TTL refresh. The SDK tokenizes and detokenizes from the in-memory codebook. At 80% of TTL elapsed, a background refresh requests a new codebook. The old codebook is held until the new one is verified and installed, so there is no gap where the SDK has no valid codebook.

Revocation and shutdown are where the DEK leaves memory.

Revocation handling. The SDK polls the revocation endpoint on the interval embedded in the codebook. A response indicating revocation (either a version-floor bump above the current codebook version or an explicit ID blocklist) triggers immediate DEK zeroing from native memory, immediate rejection of subsequent operations, and a forced re-authentication cycle. The revocation response is itself Ed25519-signed.

Shutdown. On graceful shutdown, the SDK zeroes the DEK from native memory, releases the mlock, and closes the audit emitter cleanly. On crash, the native memory allocation is released by the OS. The DEK cannot persist to swap because it was mlocked. It cannot persist to a core dump because the allocation is outside the JVM heap the dump captures.

The wrapped DEK store is its own component, with its own operational profile.

What it is. A key metadata store, separate from CloudHSM and separate from the application database. It holds wrapped DEKs: ciphertext keyed by tenant, domain, and version. CloudHSM holds only the KEKs, and holds them non-exportably. The wrapped DEK store is how the control plane assembles a codebook without asking the HSM to unwrap every DEK the platform has ever issued.

Why it deserves its own attention. The wrapped DEK store is less sensitive than CloudHSM because its contents are already encrypted. It is still not negligible. Losing it is an availability catastrophe: all tokenized data becomes permanently inaccessible even though no confidentiality breach has occurred. It needs its own access control, its own audit log of reads, and its own backup and DR posture, at the same tier as the most critical data it protects.

use(ssn);

After · tokenize on write, detokenize only when needed

// onboard a customer
String tok = sdk.tokenize(SSN, intake.ssn());
repo.save(new Customer(id, tok));

// read the customer
Customer c = repo.findById(id);
try (Plaintext pt =
     sdk.detokenize(SSN, c.ssn(), purpose)) {
  use(pt.asString());
}

The SOR stores the token. Plaintext enters at intake, is tokenized before persistence, and reappears only inside a purpose-scoped block on the read path. Every other caller of the repository sees a token and nothing else.

Appendix B · Security

Guardrails and auditing.

Access control is enforced at four layers, each independent.

  1. Certificate and client identity
    • Every consuming application is provisioned with a client certificate. The certificate resolves to a client ID. No username or password. No shared secrets. Certificate revocation at the control plane CA immediately prevents codebook issuance.
  2. Tenant and client ID check inside the codebook
    • The codebook carries tenant ID and client ID in the signed envelope. The SDK hard-rejects if the calling application's identity does not match. This defends against a codebook being transplanted to the wrong application.
  3. Policy enforcement inside the codebook
    • Per-domain operations are declared in the signed policy block. Tokenize-only clients cannot detokenize. Batch size limits are enforced locally. Purpose codes, where configured, are required before detokenize is permitted.
  4. DEK wrapping AAD binding
    • The codebook ID is used as additional authenticated data when wrapping the DEK. A DEK unwrapped outside its originating codebook fails integrity verification. Transplant attacks do not work even if an attacker obtains both a codebook and an unrelated wrapped DEK.

Longevity · Post-Quantum Posture

Shor breaks our asymmetric primitives. Grover does not reach AES-256.

  1. Shor's algorithm · breaks asymmetric
    • Polynomial-time factoring and discrete logarithm on a sufficiently large fault-tolerant quantum computer. That breaks RSA, ECDSA, Ed25519, ECDH, and Diffie-Hellman as signature and key-exchange primitives. Every asymmetric primitive in the platform is in scope for replacement: codebook signatures, mTLS certificates, and any RSA paths in the AWS KMS integration.
  2. Grover's algorithm · weakens symmetric
    • Square-root speedup on symmetric key search, which halves effective security. AES-256 retains 128-bit quantum security, well above the safe threshold. AES-128 would not. Every symmetric primitive the platform uses is AES-256, by choice, so Grover does not change the confidentiality posture.
  3. Where the platform is exposed, and where it is not
    • Tokenized data confidentiality rests on AES-256 under FF1 and AES-256 KEK wrapping inside CloudHSM. Harvest-now-decrypt-later attacks against the wrapped DEK store do not yield plaintext, because the wrapping is symmetric. Exposure is entirely on the asymmetric surfaces. A signature forgery does not expose past plaintext; it lets a future attacker try to issue unauthorized codebooks, which the version floor and HSM-anchored audit trail still constrain.
  4. Mitigation · crypto-agility now, PQC migration on NIST's cadence
    • The signature algorithm is a named field in the signed envelope, not a hardcoded assumption. Adding ML-DSA (FIPS 204) is a schema extension and an SDK update, not a rearchitecture. When PKI supports it, the control plane issues dual-signed codebooks (Ed25519 + ML-DSA) during a transition window; the SDK requires both to verify; then Ed25519 is retired. Existing tokens are unaffected because their confidentiality rests on AES, not on signatures.

Appendix C · Boundaries

Where data is allowed to flow.

Context · Why Boundaries Are Cryptographic

Boundaries between environments and consumers are enforced by keys, not by policy.

Every regulated data platform eventually confronts the same three questions. Can a non-prod environment load a copy of prod data? Can an external vendor process regulated fields on our behalf? Can the analytics team query customer records without seeing plaintext? The codebook model lets each of these boundaries be enforced by cryptography. Different consumers receive different codebooks with different capabilities, and a capability not granted in the codebook cannot be exercised by the consumer.

The platform distinguishes three consumer classes, and treats each differently.

Prod consumers · Full codebook
  • Tokenize and detokenize on the domains the client is authorized for. Short TTL, frequent refresh. This is the service-of-record pattern: the system that owns the customer relationship holds the codebook that sees plaintext.
Non-prod consumers · Tokenize-only or read-only codebook
  • Dev, test, staging, and QA environments receive codebooks that do not include the detokenize capability for sensitive fields. Data flows in as tokens from prod migration pipelines. Non-prod never sees plaintext. Lower environments cannot accidentally leak what they cannot access.
External vendors · Scoped codebook, narrow field, short TTL
  • Vendors that process regulated data on our behalf receive codebooks scoped to the exact fields they need, with TTLs measured in hours rather than days, and with detokenize volume caps enforced by policy. The vendor's codebook cannot reach any other field class or tenant.

Default posture. Analytics and reporting systems join, aggregate, and analyze on tokens. Appendix D · The Pipeline

One transformation, four triggers.

Insight · One Pipeline, Every Transformation

Emergency rotation, scheduled rotation, historical migration, and crypto-shred are the same operation.

They all read every record under one key state and write it under another. Emergency rotation reads tokens under a compromised DEK and writes them under a fresh one. Scheduled rotation does the same on a cadence. Historical migration reads plaintext and writes tokens. Crypto-shred reads tokens, destroys the DEK, and leaves the ciphertext permanently unrecoverable. Building the pipeline once, correctly, audited, and resumable means every future state transition becomes a configuration of it rather than a new project.

Architecture · The Transformation Pipeline

All four data transformation triggers use the same pipeline, built once, hardened once.

The four triggers use the same pipeline with different inputs and different policies.

  1. Scheduled rotation
    • Routine. Monthly or quarterly cadence per field class. Pipeline reads tokens under DEK version N, writes under version N+1. Version floor advances on completion. The old DEK is retained for a grace period, then destroyed.
  2. Emergency rotation (break glass)
    • Compromise-driven. Same pipeline, compressed timeline. The version floor advances before the rotation completes, so the compromised DEK is revoked at the issuance boundary while the pipeline is still reading records. The old DEK remains in the wrapped store for the detokenize continuity window, then destroyed.
  3. Historical migration
    • Plaintext in, tokens out. The only input-adapter difference from rotation is that the source is not yet tokenized. Same guarantees: no intermediate plaintext copies, atomic per-record, resumable. Phase 2 capability.
  4. Crypto-shred audit
    • Tenant offboarding or right-to-erasure. The shred itself is KEK or DEK destruction; the pipeline is not strictly required. The pipeline is used to verify the shred's scope, confirming that affected data matches the declared scope before the destruction is logged as complete.
Triggers · What Forces A New Codebook

Four categories of change force a new codebook. The signature covers all four.

Key state change
  • DEK rotation under the same KEK, activation of a new field-class DEK, or KEK rotation in CloudHSM. The wrapped DEK in the envelope is invalidated; the codebook is re-issued under the new key state.
Policy change
  • Operation permissions (tokenize-only → tokenize+detokenize), batch-size limits, purpose-code requirements, or per-domain field-class scope. The signed policy block changes; nothing else has to.
Identity or scope change
  • Tenant ID, client ID, or the set of authorized domains for a client. Identity sits inside the signed envelope and is verified against the calling application; a scope change is always a re-issuance.
Expiry or revocation
  • Routine TTL refresh at 80% elapsed, version-floor bump after rotation, or explicit revocation of a single client. Each path produces a fresh signed codebook (or a signed revocation response) with a new validity window.

Change any part — key, policy, identity, or expiry — and the Ed25519 signature fails. Re-issuance is the only valid response.

The pipeline has four non-negotiable guarantees.

  1. No new plaintext at rest
    • The pipeline streams. Plaintext never lands in an intermediate store, a staging table, or a logfile. Records are read, transformed in-memory under the appropriate DEK, and written under the target DEK. Failure mid-stream leaves the source unchanged.
  2. Atomic per-record
    • Every record either completes its transformation or is untouched. There is no half-transformed state. A record that fails transformation stays on the source side of the boundary and is logged for operator review.
  3. Resumable after failure
    • The pipeline maintains a checkpoint of the last committed record per source partition. A crash, timeout, or operator-initiated stop can be resumed from the checkpoint without reprocessing committed records or skipping uncommitted ones.
  4. Audited at bulk and record granularity
    • Every pipeline invocation is logged at the control plane with scope, trigger, operator, and completion state. Every record processed is logged at a lower-granularity trail that supports examiner "show me every record in scope" questions without exposing plaintext in the audit log.

Emergency rotation, walked through end to end.

The trigger and the first five minutes. Compromise is declared. A compromised credential, a leaked DEK, a client certificate that should not have issued. The on-call engineer opens the control plane's emergency rotation flow. The new DEK is generated in CloudHSM. The version floor for the affected tenant and field class is raised to the new version. Every active codebook below the floor is now invalid for new operations. The compromise is contained at the issuance boundary, immediately, before the rotation pipeline even starts.

The pipeline and the detokenize continuity window. The rotation pipeline reads tokens under the old DEK and writes them under the new DEK, streaming. The old DEK remains in the wrapped store but not in any issuable codebook. Detokenize of tokens written before the rotation completes resolves to the old DEK version from the token's embedded version field. The SDK reaches through the wrapped store to the HSM for unwrap, but only for codebooks that the control plane has explicitly authorized for emergency detokenize. Completion of the pipeline closes this window, and the old DEK is scheduled for destruction.

The examiner conversation opens with custody and compromise.

"Where does your key material live?" In CloudHSM, non-exportable, partitioned per tenant. The KEK has never left the hardware since its creation. Every unwrap operation is logged inside the HSM, and that log stands independent of any platform component being honest.

"What happens when an application is compromised?" The application holds a codebook with a wrapped DEK, a policy block, and a signature. Compromise yields those three artifacts. The KEK remains in the HSM, untouched. Revocation raises the version floor, and the compromised codebook becomes invalid for new operations within minutes. We have a documented playbook, and we rehearse it on a cadence.

Rotation and audit completeness have documented answers.

"How do you rotate keys?" Version-floor rotation through the transformation pipeline. The pipeline is used for scheduled rotation, emergency rotation, and historical migration. One code path for all three, which means it runs frequently enough to stay hardened. We can show a rotation that completed last month and the examiner-grade audit trail for it.

"How do you know your audit logs are complete?" Three layers. HSM audit is tamper-evident and lives outside our control. Control plane audit lives inside the platform's trust boundary but is centralized and queryable. SDK local audit lives inside the consuming application and is not trusted for tamper-evidence by design. We reconcile against HSM audit. Any delta between SDK-reported operations and HSM-logged unwraps is investigated.

The closing questions are about scope and separation.

"Show me the records you protect." Per-tenant, per-field-class scope is declared in the codebook schema and enforced by the control plane at issuance. We can produce the set of field classes under tokenization per tenant, and the audit trail of what was tokenized when. Historical migration is tracked through the transformation pipeline's record-level audit, which answers scope-of-coverage questions at record granularity.

"Who can do what?" Three roles across three trust boundaries, with no overlap. The HSM admin manages KEK lifecycle and has no access to the wrapped DEK store or the control plane's signing keys. The platform operator manages policy, client provisioning, and revocation, and has no access to HSM credentials. The consuming application holds only time-bound signed codebooks and has no path to either. No single role has custody of plaintext key material.
